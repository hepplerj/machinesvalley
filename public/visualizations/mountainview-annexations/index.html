Mountain View Annexations, 1950—1990

Mountain View underwent dramatic expansion like many of the Bay Area cities in the postwar era.

About the Map

Postwar Mountain View underwent rapid expansion. While not as aggressive as San Jose, the city grew rapidly as it absorbed new homeowners and businesses.

Mountain View remains a place in high-tech lore: the famous restaurant, Walker’s Wagon Wheel, was a popular hangout for Fairchild Semiconductor employees along Whisman Road, where many companies were founded. (The journalist Don Hoefler, credited with putting the nickname “Silicon Valley” into print for the first time, referred to his barstool at the Wagon Wheel as his “field office.”)1 High-tech fabrication and manufacturing congregated here, including Fairchild Semiconductor, GTE, Intel, Mitsubishi, NEC, and Raytheon, all of which also took advantage of their close proximity to Moffett Airfield nearby. Mountain View was a key site for semiconductor manufacturing throughout the twentieth century and, incidentally, became one of the most polluted as well.
